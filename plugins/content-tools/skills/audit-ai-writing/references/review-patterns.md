# Review Patterns

Use these patterns as editorial evidence, not proof of AI authorship.

## High-Signal Problems

- The opening gives broad context but delays the answer the reader needs.
- Advice remains balanced or generic instead of ranking the real tradeoffs.
- Claims use `important`, `significant`, `many`, `best`, or causal verbs without
  a defined actor, timeframe, denominator, source, or consequence.
- Paragraphs repeat the same cadence, transition, rhetorical contrast, or
  synthetic concluding clause.
- Text contains tool identifiers, citation placeholders, chatbot preambles,
  tracking parameters, broken Markdown references, invented headings, or markup
  for the wrong destination.
- A quote, name, number, date, title, URL, DOI, or bibliography record disagrees
  with the cited source.

## Repair Moves

- State the answer or consequence before background.
- Name the reader's decision, constraint, objection, and next action.
- Replace abstract praise with actors, actions, dates, examples, and limits.
- Replace vague attribution with a named source or remove it.
- Downgrade causation to chronology or correlation unless direct evidence
  supports the stronger claim.
- Replace relative dates with absolute dates or explicit versions.
- Delete residue and empty scaffolding rather than paraphrasing it.
- Attach each citation to the exact claim it supports and split compound claims
  when support differs.

## Weak Evidence

Do not elevate these alone into findings about provenance: perfect grammar,
formal prose, one em dash, one transition word, multilingual English, a
three-item list, or a detector score. Judge clusters in the context of genre,
audience, translation, and house style.
